Week 1: The Logic of Objects

The Logic of Objects

Maria Martinez. Image sequence of Maria's process. Mexican. 1930s. Photography.

  • Cooke coined the term, the idea to look deeply at the object itself, and not what we believe it to be. Process by which to understand an object.
  • To understand an object on its own terms, begin with materials and techniques of fabrication and decoration, rather than project an ahistorical interpretation based on contemporary taste of prejudice.
  • The focus on paying attention to what one sees and analyzes visually, rather than judging on meaning and culmination.
  • Emphasis on materiality, realization, and then interchange.

Hot water plate. Jingdezhen, China. 1830-70. Molded porcelain with underglaze cobalt blue painted decoration.

Wedging clay, Shaping on the Potter's Wheel, Molding and Refining. Guangzhou, China, 1825. Gouache on paper.

Bowl with Anthropomorphic Cacao Trees. Maya. Early Classic Period (400-500 CE). Carbonate Stone.

Maria Martinez and Julian Martinez. Jar. ca. 1939; Blackware.

Nabhan, Cumins, Camels, and Caravans: A Spice Odyssey

  • Spices have driven global trade: Frankincese is tied both to cultural and personal memory (Taking advantage of the one thing the desert had to give).
  • Spices have a really important place in the development of cultures and interchange.

Cooke, Jr., Global Objects: Toward a Connected Art History

  • Logic of Objects. Fabrication -> Decoration -> finish. Steps of realization.
  • The common materials are clay (breaks down further), fiber (breaks down plant/animal), and wood.
  • View the selection of materials as a choice that requires cultural and technical experience and expertise.
  • Objects are complex assemblages of ideas, materials, and performances that traveled from centers to peripheries, from the edge of the empire back to the metropole.

Week 2: Chocolate

Cacao and Chocolatero

Unknown. Chocolate Jar (Chocolatero). Mexican Puebla. ca 1700. Tin-glazed earthenware, iron.

  • Cacao:
    • Mesoamerican word. Powerful good heavily traded and visualized in Maya culture. Contains amphetamines and other stimulants.
    • Developed originally by the Olmecs. Needs a humid and warm environment.
    • Anthropomorphized as deities, notions of rebirth and cycles. Used as currency.
    • Many vessels showed stories, inscriptions, gods, and the use of Cacao in culture, as a drink or other forms.
  • Chocolatero:
    • Chocolate jars. Sometimes with iron lids that locked, given cacao was valuable.
    • Puebla style of earthenware mixed porcelain-like elements: blue and white colors, but with Mexican imagery, like Quetzal birds.
    • Used for the storage and containing of chocolate and chocolate beans.

Unknown. Chocolate Jar (Chocolatero). Mexican Puebla. ca 1700. Tin-glazed earthenware, iron.

Coe, The True History of Chocolate

  • Word "cacao" comes from Mayan language. Although first founded by Olmec, the Mayans were first to make an elaborate drinking and food culture out of cacao.
  • Cacao and blood were both offerings to the gods, with specific recipes for the gods vs daily consumption.
  • Cacao had a strong place in deities and symbolism -> cyclic of rebirth and renewal. Also used as currency in Mayan culture.

Priyadarshini, Chinese Porcelain in Colonial Mexico

  • Puebla potters combined indigenous and chinese pottery styles european/hispano designs (Talavera Poblana).
  • Cholatero (for holding cacao) based on Chinese guan. Blue and white style and shape but with local images and lid modified to prevent theft of cacao.
  • Puebla potters were not only making for export but were connected to the ideas that went on the pottery, unlike potters in China that were making for mass production.

Week 3: Dyes and Pigments

Tie-Dyed Lahiry-a turban clothT Tie-dyed cotton. India (Rajasthan) 1840s-1870s.

Houghteling, “Dyeing the Springtime"

  • Fugitive dyes: vibrant dyes that fade fast once washed.
  • Like love and passion, strongly present yet fades with time. Fever of devotion.
  • Important place in culture, kachcha ̄ turbans and shawls were worn in the rain, in Rajasthan to celebrate the monsoon festival so their colors would run together and then be redyed for the next season.

Phipps, "Global Colors: Dyes and the Dye Trade"

  • A huge part of the wealth in the global trade network of the 16-18th century was not just silver/gold/silk, but dyes and colorants.
  • Dye woods, cochineal reds, indigo (and woad) blue The americas and india had lots of plants and bugs with a lot more dye in them, which revolutionized how many people can have died clothing.
  • Cotton and linen were also imported and so much lighter than flax/hemp.

Week 4: Sugar and Refinement

After Giovanni Battista Zaccarini (sugar sculptor), Trionfi (sugar sculptures), etching, from Disegni del convito (1693).

William Clark. Ten View in the Island of Antigua. 1823.

Mintz, Sweetness and Power

  • Europeans and Americans transformed sugar from a rare foreign luxury to a commonplace necessity of modern life, and how it changed the history of capitalism and industry.
  • Sugar and colonialism are so intertwined and present an important insight into power dynamics, but because they're European anthropologists often won't touch it.
  • Sugar was a display of wealth, an imported good in Europe developed on the labor of slaves.

Barringer, "Picturesque Prospects and the Labor of the Enslaved"

  • Scenes of labor and slavery were "painted over" with picturesque naturalization: livestock, water, winding paths.
  • Reassures investors that their property is taken care of (both people and land), and that the sugar is moving out.
  • The picturesque itself, as a form, was subject to an analogous instability, always tending to reveal that which it attempted to conceal. The harder it strove to naturalize artificial social relations—such as slavery—the more clearly the shallow artifice of the picturesque was revealed.

Week 5: Still Life

Still Life

Clara Peeters. Still Life with Cheese, Almonds, and Pretzels. Flemish. 1615. Oil on canvas.

  • Barkan describes still life as "objects that don't move of speak, a visual entity made from a group of objects, nothing altering design, assemblage".
  • "A deliberate arraignment."
  • A representation of food (statistically, the most) or objects, but often containing a stronger symbolism: mimesis and often painted with verisimilitude depicting food exactly how it is.
  • Accessible paintings because they are often of familiar objects.
  • Took different forms:
    • Xenia (Greek attempt at photo realism), hospitality.
    • Stilleven, Dutch dramaticized displays of patriotism.
    • Bodegon, Spanish monastic works. High contrast, anorexic.

Clara Peeters. Still Life with Cheese, Almonds, and Pretzels. Flemish. 1615. Oil on canvas.

Juan Sánchez Cotán. Still Life with Fruit and Vegetables. 1602. Hernani Collection, Madrid.

Unknown. Peaches and a Glass Jug. Roman, Herculaeneum. ca 50BC.

Barkan, The Hungry Eye

  • Xenia are pictures of food. Alongside the dinner and food sent home with guests in this culture of hospitality there existed a genre of visual representations memorializing those edible gifts.
  • Pictorial photo-realism of the real xenia suits perfectly with the underlying thematic of ekphrasis.
  • Still life is a painting of items, very deliberately arranged to create a scene: an assemblage.

Hochstrasser, Still Life and Trade

  • Dutch stilleven showed off the technical prowess of Dutch / Flemish painters, mixed with the exotic and ostentatious (like citrus).
  • Great emphasis on capitalism and patriotism in Dutch still life. Abundance of the exotic mixed with exports and national identities of the Dutch, like cheese.

Bryson, Looking at the Overlooked

  • Spanish still life was very contrasted in contereros. Dark backgrounds created bare, geometric scenes.
  • Monastic pieces showing off a lack of food. Anorexic in display.

Week 6: Sacred and Profane Kitchens and Gardens

Charbagh

  • Literally translates to "four garden". Divided by two intersecting channels of water to create quadrants of cultivation.
  • Fountain in the middle. Lined by walls, arcades, and usually a pavilion across the lines on the sides.
  • Small lots of plants arranged geometrically, big focus on mosaic, tile. Mix of trees and plants.
  • Serence oaises in the middle of barren regions, to be walked through peacefully or be a scenic place.

Jan Steen. The Fat and Lean Kitchen. Between 1650 and 1655. Oil on panel.

Leonardo da Vinci. Last Supper. 1490s. Santa Maria della Grazie in Milan.

Varriano, Tastes and Temptations

  • There is actually very little about the food in Bible, either for the last supper or for when risen christ visits his disciples.
  • The food present often reflects more about the artist and their local food culture/personal taste. Especially in the Renaissance when it was the perfect humanist and symmetrical thing to paint.
  • Leonardo's last supper used anachronistic food-> eels, which were popular during his time. Temporal touches that could be related to.

Ruggles, Islamic Gardens and Landscapes

  • During the Islamic empire's reign, 600-700s CE, manmade oasises lead to new agriculture and expression, using irrigation.
  • Because gardens are ephemeral, plant lists and the occasional archaeological dig are the best resources we have.
  • Beautifully arranged gardens as escapes for the wealthy in their complexes.

Week 7: Spaces of Tea and Coffee

Wabi Tea

  • Rustic and simple places, full of ritual and cultural heritage. Founded on the Wabi styles by Rikyu.
  • Measured by number of mats in the house, usually relatively small. Had to crawl to enter under small door. Different entrances for guests and host. Choreographed ceremony from start to end.
  • Mizuya was water preparation room.
  • Tokonoma was raised alcove with hanging scroll and flower arrangement (ancestral area). Central hearth to brew the tea.

Ottoman Coffeehouse

  • New social houses, for people to go gather and escape the home. Stimulated by coffee.
  • Courtyard structure, fountain in the middle with raised platform (Divan) around it.
  • Separate sitting areas that overlook the main room for intimate gatherings (the top Divan).
  • Very much a social center. A lot of seating room around, often with an open fireplace (the semi circle).

Black Seto Teabowl, known as "Iron Mallet". Momoyama Period. Late 16th century. Japan. Glazed stoneware.

Ottoman painting showing the interior of a coffeehouse. 1610.

Ohki, Tea Culture of Japan

  • The practice of chanoyu is a dynamic and multidisensional activity that encompases many art form and all the human senses.
  • Wabi tea and it champion Rikyu, may be regarded a an antidote to the extravagant excesses of basara tea.

Hozumi, What is Japanese Architecture?

  • The tea ceremony (chanoyü) was one of the most important upper-class entertainments in the late Muromachi, Momoyama, and Edo periods, and it continues to be practiced by millions today.
  • Wabi tea houses were extremely rustic, deeply endowed in ceremony and ritual. Simplistic and small houses.
  • Rikyu inspired ideals of the imperfect -> people collected uneven and "shotty" cups and other tools to add to their collection. Rustic, and away from the idea of refinement. Bamboo tools.

Ergin, “And in the soup kitchen food shall be cooked twice every day:”

  • Food is tied to architecture, especially for religious buildings such as mosques/churchs where there were soup kitchens, but also restaurants and inns.

Hattox, Coffee and Coffee Houses: The Origins of a Social Beverage in the Medieval Near East

  • Coffee was a primarily a social drink, at first in Yemen in rituals, and then in the Ottoman world as coffee houses as a place to get away from the home. This was not limited to men, tho women only got one day reserved for them.

Week 8: The Restaurant and the Market

Vincent Van Gogh, Interior of a Restaurant. 1887, Paris.

Caillebotte. Langouste a la Parisien. 1881. Paris. Oil on Canvas.

Spang, The Invention of the Restaurant

  • Originally a tiny bowl of bouillon (restaurant), a restorative broth (A medical term).
  • Then became a place to drink the consomme. Until mid 1800s, restaurants were solely a Parisian phenomenon.
  • Nouvelle Cuisine was a trend of making food more about health -> the delicacies and intricacies of food, preparation, and display became important. Culinary refinement.

Marrinan, Gustave Caillebotte

  • Caillebotte DOES NOT SHARE Zola's implied critique of modern consumer culture.
  • Fruit Displayed on a Stand's perspective mimics the view of someone walking past, with things painted at an angle: Caillebotte is less interested in the fruit than the city dweller's experience of fruit, as a luxury.

Zola, The Belly of Paris

  • Food can be seen as art, this is a new idea in the early 19th/late 19th century, and relates to the rise of nouvelle cuisine and food as more than nutrition.
